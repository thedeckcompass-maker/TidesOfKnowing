## Post copy

Part 6 of the AI and Intuition series asks a harder question than most technology debates allow.

Not whether AI is useful.
Not whether practitioners should resist change.
But whether your practice still begins with your own noticing.

Augmented intuition keeps the practitioner upstream of the tool. Replaced thinking is quieter. It arrives when interpretation feels easier than perception, when a plausible summary arrives before you have named what you actually saw in the spread, the situation, or your own inner response.

Perception gives specificity. Interpretation alone gives plausible generality. That distinction sounds academic until you recognise how often generic output feels satisfying precisely because it bypasses the slower work of trained perception.

The useful sequence remains steady: soft eyes first, structural observation second, AI consulted last if at all, only as comparison, challenge, language support or bias checking. AI must not become the interpretive authority. It must not move upstream of the practitioner's noticing.

Tarot is the visible practice in this piece. The deeper issue is symbolic literacy in an age of instant interpretation.
