    1/ The real tension in AI and intuitive practice is not whether the tool is good or bad. It is whether you are practising augmented intuition or replaced thinking. The distinction is sequence.

    2/ Augmented intuition keeps the practitioner upstream of the tool. Replaced thinking lets the interpretive layer arrive before you have done the perceptual work. That drift is gradual, and it is easy to miss.

    3/ The useful sequence is simple: soft eyes first, structural observation second, AI consulted last, if at all, as a comparison or bias-checking layer. Perception before interpretation. Human noticing before algorithmic plausibility.

    4/ Perception gives specificity. Interpretation alone gives plausible generality. When AI answers before you have named what you actually saw, the output may feel accurate while bypassing the work that trains discernment.

    5/ In an AI-saturated world, the practitioner's edge is the capacity to perceive what tools cannot originate. That edge is not anti-technology. It is pro-attention, pro-sequence and pro-human knowing.

    6/ Tarot is the visible practice here, but the principle is broader: symbolic literacy depends on trained perception, not on faster interpretation. The question is whether your practice still begins with your own noticing.

    7/ Read the full article on augmented intuition versus replaced thinking:
