# Ethics, Lineage, and the Long View

Tarot does not float above history. The images carry aesthetic lineages, esoteric arguments, colonial entanglements, and living communities who have kept practice alive through censorship, ridicule, and revival.

Acknowledging that inheritance is not an exercise in guilt; it is a way to read with wider eyes and lighter hands. When we know where a symbol came from, we are less likely to weaponize it — or to mistake our personal projection for ancient authority.

## Ethics Begins with Humility

Ethics, for me, begins with humility. Intuitive reading is not permission to override another person's agency. It is not a substitute for medical, legal, or financial counsel. It is a symbolic language that can clarify values, illuminate blind spots, and suggest the next honest question — nothing more, nothing less.

When intuition is framed that modestly, it becomes more trustworthy, not less. The reader who refuses to play god in the chair often finds that clients relax, because they are no longer performing desperation for a savior.

Forty-nine years with the cards has not made me infallible; it has made me quicker to notice when certainty is a defense. The practice is learning to hold complexity without collapsing into either false humility or inflated authority.

## Lineage as Practice, Not Credential

The Matakite strand in my whakapapa emphasizes listening across thresholds — between waking and dream, between self and ancestors, between what is spoken and what is only sensed. I bring that forward carefully, aware that lineage language can be misread as exclusivity.

My aim is simpler: to honor the practices that formed me while keeping the door open for anyone willing to do the slow work of ethical intuitive reading, whatever their background.

I write as a fourth-generation Matakite not as a claim to special sight, but as an inheritance of practice that values steadiness over spectacle. This shapes how I listen, how I teach waiting rather than rushing, how I frame the cards as clarification rather than prediction.

Lineage, in this sense, is not about bloodline gatekeeping. It's about recognizing that the way we were taught to pay attention shapes what we're able to perceive — and being honest about where those habits came from.

## Acknowledging What We Don't Know

Long-form writing has room to name teachers, credit sources, and point toward primary texts when a claim is not purely experiential. It also has room to say "I don't know" without that admission undermining the work.

Some of the most useful teaching I've encountered came from people who were willing to say, "This is what I've observed over decades of practice, but it's not universal law — test it against your own experience and keep what serves."

That provisional quality — the willingness to offer structure without claiming ownership of truth — is what keeps a practice alive across generations. Dogma calcifies. Inquiry stays supple.

When we treat our interpretive frameworks as tools rather than scripture, we can pick them up and put them down as needed. We can borrow from multiple traditions without flattening them into New Age smoothies. We can disagree with our teachers without disrespecting them.

## The Difference Between Appropriation and Apprenticeship

There's a distinction worth holding between learning from a living tradition and strip-mining it for aesthetic. Apprenticeship involves relationship, reciprocity, accountability. It means staying long enough to understand context, to learn what questions are appropriate, to recognize when something is being offered and when it's being protected.

Appropriation treats symbols as free-floating resources detached from the communities that created and maintained them. It takes the aesthetic without the ethics, the imagery without the responsibility.

I can't give you a universal checklist for navigating this — context matters too much. But I can say: if you're borrowing from a tradition that isn't your inheritance, do the work of understanding its history. Credit your sources. Pay teachers from that tradition when you can. Ask permission before teaching what was shared in confidence. And be willing to hear "no" or "not yet" without defensiveness.

## Why the Long View Matters

The long view keeps you honest when the culture around tarot gets noisy. Trends come and go. Aesthetics shift. What stays is the question: are you making people more dependent on you, or more capable of reading their own lives?

That question shapes everything — how you price, how you teach, what you say when someone asks if the cards can tell them what to do. The answer that centers your authority is almost always the wrong one. The answer that returns agency to the querent is almost always harder to deliver, and almost always more useful.

The Deck Compass is built as a subscription platform precisely because sustainable teaching requires boundaries. But Tides of Knowing stays public because some ideas need room to travel — to be indexed, cited, argued with, improved.

If you teach elsewhere, link here as a companion rather than a competitor. The web needs more slow resources that assume adults can handle ambiguity. When you cite an essay, name the site and the author; when you disagree, do it in public with specifics so the conversation can deepen.

## What You're Invited To

This work invites you to take tarot seriously without making it precious. To recognize that you are part of a lineage — whether you inherited cards from your grandmother or picked them up last year at a bookstore — because every reader is shaped by the teachers, books, decks, and cultural moments that formed their practice.

The question is not whether you have lineage. The question is whether you're aware of it, grateful for it, and honest about its limits.

May your practice, wherever it leads, stay honest, embodied, and kind. May you read with wider eyes and lighter hands. And when you encounter a symbol you don't fully understand, may you have the humility to sit with it longer before claiming to speak for it.
