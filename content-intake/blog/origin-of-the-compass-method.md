I've been reading cards for more than 40 years.

I took my first tarot deck to primary school. I probably shouldn't have — but I did, and something about the cards made immediate sense to me in a way that almost nothing else did at that age.

I didn't know what I was doing. I didn't have a teacher or a book that explained it properly. I just read.

And for decades, that's what I kept doing.

I read for friends, for clients, for myself. I read through uncertainty, grief, major life shifts, and stretches of profound clarity. I read well sometimes. Other times I read poorly and didn't know why.

But that was only one part of my life.

---

## Two parallel worlds

While I was developing as an intuitive, I was also working as a journalist.

And journalism is about as black-and-white as you can get. You deal with facts. What happened. Who said what. What's verifiable. What's provable. What sits directly in front of you.

For years, I lived in both worlds simultaneously.

In one, I worked with cards, intuition, signal, and perception that couldn't always be explained or defended logically.

In the other, I worked with evidence, sources, deadlines, and the discipline of removing myself from the story so I could report it accurately.

Most of the time, those worlds stayed separate.

But sometimes, they crossed over.

---

## The police beat

When I was a police reporter at the Otago Daily Times in Dunedin, New Zealand, I started noticing something I couldn't ignore.

When people went missing, I would instinctively know whether they were alive or dead.

Not always immediately. But the knowing would arrive — clear, quiet, undeniable.

I'd know if they would be found. Sometimes I'd know when. Not with precision down to the hour, but with enough accuracy that I started trusting it.

And here's what mattered: I had no vested interest in the outcome.

I wasn't emotionally attached. I wasn't hoping for one result over another. I was reporting, not involved. That distance — that removal by one layer — meant I could perceive without distortion.

There was no fear clouding the signal. No wishful thinking. No urgency to make the answer be something I wanted it to be.

Just signal. And I learned to trust it.

That's where the first stirrings of what would eventually become the COMPASS Method came from.

---

## What journalism taught me about intuition

Journalism gave me something most intuitive practitioners don't get early on: a framework for observing without interference.

In reporting, you learn to:

- arrive without agenda  
- observe what's actually there, not what you expect  
- distinguish between signal and noise  
- check your assumptions before you commit to a narrative  
- stay with ambiguity until clarity forms organically  

Sound familiar?

Those are the same conditions that make intuitive reading reliable.

The difference is that in journalism, those skills are formalised, practiced, and required. You can't submit a story based on what you felt might be true. You have to observe, verify, and stay grounded in what's actually present.

I realised that intuition worked the same way.

When I removed my attachment to the outcome — when I had no stake in what the answer should be — my perception became sharper. More stable. More trustworthy.

But when I was reading for myself, or for someone I cared about, or in a situation where I wanted a particular result, that clarity collapsed.

Not because my intuition failed.

But because the conditions that allow intuition to function clearly weren't there.

---

## The structure started forming

I began identifying what had to be present for a reading to stay stable.

Not steps. Not a process I followed linearly.

But qualities of attention that had to exist — sometimes simultaneously, sometimes in sequence — for clarity to hold.

I noticed that readings collapsed when I skipped certain things:

- arriving too quickly, already halfway into interpretation before I'd even registered what was in front of me  
- forcing meaning too early because I wanted certainty or didn't want to sit with ambiguity  
- failing to observe the whole spread before zeroing in on one card  
- letting my projection take over without checking whether what I was sensing was signal or just what I wanted to see  

And I noticed that readings stayed coherent when I did the opposite:

- centered before starting  
- stayed open to what was actually there  
- mapped the whole field first  
- perceived pattern before assigning meaning  
- aligned with clarity instead of urgency  
- let meaning consolidate without rushing it  
- sealed the reading properly so it didn't keep circulating in my head  

These weren't random. They formed a structure.

And they mapped almost perfectly onto the disciplines I'd learned as a journalist.

That structure became the COMPASS Method.

---

## Why I finally formalised it

For a long time, I just used this internally. It worked for me, and that was enough.

But as I started teaching — first informally, then within more structured learning environments as a tertiary educator — I realised something else:

**Intuition can be taught. But only if there's a framework that makes it learnable.**

You can't teach someone to "just trust their intuition" and expect that to work reliably. Intuition isn't magic. It's perception. And perception can be refined, stabilised, and made consistent if you understand the conditions that allow it to function clearly.

That's what the COMPASS Method does.

It doesn't replace intuition. It creates the conditions that make intuition usable across time, across different contexts, and under pressure.

I formalised it because people needed it. And because after four decades of figuring this out slowly — across two parallel careers, through thousands of readings and hundreds of stories — I didn't want others to have to spend 40 years doing the same thing.

---

## What the COMPASS Method actually is

COMPASS stands for seven conditions of attention that stabilise intuitive reading:

**C — Center**: Presence before perception  
**O — Open**: Receptivity without agenda  
**M — Map**: Observation before interpretation  
**P — Perceive**: Signal before story  
**A — Align**: Checking for distortion  
**S — Sense**: Integration without forcing language  
**S — Seal**: Closure and containment  

These aren't steps you follow mechanically. They're qualities of awareness that must be present for signal to stay stable during a reading.

When they're there, readings don't collapse under pressure. You stop second-guessing yourself constantly. You can distinguish between what the cards are showing and what you're projecting onto them.

The cards don't change. What changes is the quality of perception you bring to them.

And that makes all the difference.

## The journalist and the intuitive

Looking back now, I can see how those two worlds informed each other.

Journalism taught me discipline, structure, and the importance of removing my attachment to outcomes.

Intuitive work taught me how to perceive what couldn't be verified through conventional means — and how to trust that perception when it was grounded in clarity rather than hope or fear.

The COMPASS Method sits at the intersection of both.

It's a framework that honors intuition without mystifying it. That makes perception reliable without reducing it to formulaic interpretation. That allows you to work with ambiguity, complexity, and uncertainty without collapsing into doubt.

And it works because it's grounded in the same disciplines that make any perceptual skill reliable: observation, presence, integrity, and the ability to distinguish between signal and projection.

## Why I'm sharing this now

I've spent years refining this framework quietly, using it in my own practice and teaching it to students who were ready for something more structured than "follow your intuition."

But I've reached a point where I want this work to exist beyond my immediate circle.

Not because I think everyone needs it — plenty of readers work brilliantly without a formal framework, and that's completely valid.

But for those who have felt that same frustration I felt for years — the sense that your intuition is there, but it's unstable, unreliable, or hard to trust when it matters most — this is for you.

The COMPASS Method is original work. I developed it over more than 40 years of practice, observation, and teaching — across two careers that taught me how perception actually functions when you remove distortion and stay grounded in what's present.

It's not derived from existing tarot traditions or psychological models. It's a synthesis of what I've learned about clarity, both in the black-and-white world of journalism and in the less tangible world of intuitive reading.

And I'm genuinely delighted to finally have it in a form that can be taught.

## Teaching the method

I've started offering structured training in the COMPASS Method because understanding the framework intellectually is not the same as being able to apply it reliably under pressure.

Reading the article on [the COMPASS Method](/articles/compass-method/) will give you the structure. You'll understand what the seven conditions are and why they matter.

But applying them in real time — when you're reading for yourself during a crisis, or for a client who's emotionally activated, or when the cards are showing something you don't want to see — that takes practice.

And that's where the training comes in.

I work with people who want to develop their intuition in a way that's grounded, consistent, and doesn't require mystification or guesswork. If that's you, I'd be happy to work with you.

## What this makes possible

When attention is held with structural consistency, intuition becomes easier to trust.

Not because it becomes infallible — it doesn't.

But because it becomes coherent.

And that coherence extends beyond readings.

It starts influencing how you make decisions, how you recognise when something's misaligned before it becomes obvious, how you hold boundaries without justification, how you stay with uncertainty without forcing premature resolution.

The framework doesn't stay contained to the cards. It becomes a condition of how you perceive.

That's the deeper function of this work.

And after 40 years of figuring it out slowly — across newsrooms and tarot decks, missing persons cases and client readings, facts and intuition — I'm genuinely excited to share it with people who are ready for it.
