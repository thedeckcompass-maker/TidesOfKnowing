If you have carried a deck for more than a season, you already understand that intuitive tarot is not a party trick. It is a discipline of attention: learning to stay with an image until it yields something truer than your first association. The [methodology articles](/articles/) are written for that kind of reader—someone who treats the cards as a conversation rather than a verdict machine. Over time, those pages will gather essays, exercises, and quiet arguments that reward rereading when the world feels loud and your practice feels thin.

The voice here is mine—Leigh Spencer. I write as a guide and founder, and as someone who has lived with tarot for forty-nine years, including the long work of sorting intuition from wishful thinking. My lineage as a fourth-generation Matakite shapes how I listen: not as a claim to special sight, but as an inheritance of practice that values steadiness over spectacle. I am not interested in convincing you that the cards predict the future. I am interested in how they clarify the present, and how honest language around symbols can restore agency instead of stealing it.

Tides of Knowing sits alongside The Deck Compass — a subscription home for intuitive readers who want structure, community, and courses that respect the craft. Here, the writing stays open and slow—partly for search, partly for the reader at midnight who needs a thoughtful companion, and partly because some ideas simply need room. Think of this site as the shelf you return to between lessons: not a replacement for practice, but a place where the why and the how can breathe together.

## Why intuitive tarot still needs structure

Structure is often mistaken for rigidity. In tarot, it is closer to a trellis: something that holds the vine while it grows in its own direction. When you know why you shuffle the way you do, why you cut, why you assign meaning to positions in a spread, your body has a rhythm to follow while your mind stays loose enough to notice what is actually there. Positions are not cages; they are coordinates. They tell your eye where to begin so your intuition has a path to walk instead of a blank field to panic in.

Readers who call themselves intuitive sometimes fear that technique will flatten their gifts. I have rarely seen that happen when technique is taught as language rather than law. Naming the difference between empathy and fusion, between a pattern in the cards and a fear in your gut, does not make you colder—it makes you kinder. You stop turning your anxiety into the querent's destiny. You learn to ask a better question before the first card is placed. Intuition that learns grammar tends to speak more clearly, not less.

The Deck Compass teaches spreads, narrative skills, and ethical frameworks in a cohort setting. This library will linger on the edges of those lessons: the essays you read when a single card keeps returning, when a boundary feels blurry, or when you need permission to read slowly in a culture that rewards hot takes. Both spaces share a premise: tarot is worthy of seriousness without becoming solemn for its own sake. Humour belongs here too, when it serves honesty.

## Reading as relationship, not performance

Most public tarot content rewards performance: rapid-fire pulls, theatrical certainty, and readings shaped for retention graphs rather than human nervous systems. There is nothing wrong with theatre when everyone consents to the stage. The trouble begins when performance replaces relationship—when the reader feels they must always sound prophetic, always land a zinger, always translate ambiguity into a headline before anyone has exhaled. Intuitive work withers under that pressure, because intuition needs slack: a pause, a second look, the willingness to say, "This image is sharp for me today; I need a moment before I put it into words."

Relationship-based reading begins with consent and context. It asks what the querent wants from the session, what they can metabolise today, and which topics need gentler handling or clear referral boundaries around health, legal matters, or other people who are not in the room. It leaves room for the querent to correct your metaphor when it does not fit their life. It treats the spread as shared inquiry rather than monologue. Those habits are easy to admire on paper and surprisingly hard to maintain when you are tired, flattered, or afraid of disappointing someone who paid for your time—which is why they deserve repeated attention in writing that is allowed to be long.

When you read for yourself, relationship still applies. You are not an enemy client; you are a person who deserves the same patience you would extend to someone you love. Self-reading often skews when an inner critic hijacks the voice of the cards, turning every draw into a referendum on your worth. The essays here will return to that tension: how to hear useful critique without cruelty, how to mark growth without bypassing grief, and how journaling and somatic awareness keep intuitive insights tethered to truth rather than fantasy.

> A spread is not a sentence passed down from the universe; it is a mirror held at an angle—and the skill lies in learning which angle still honours the person looking in.

The cards will challenge you. They will occasionally embarrass you by naming what you hoped to skip. The practice is not to win every reading; it is to remain trustworthy across hundreds of small moments when nobody is watching. That trust is built in the boring work: note-taking, spread drills, apologising when you miss, revisiting a reading a week later to see what shifted. Performance wants applause. Relationship wants repair. This library sides with the second.

Performance also warps teaching. When instructors model only flawless reads, students learn to hide uncertainty instead of metabolise it. I will write from the middle of the work—where meanings sometimes arrive half-formed and language lags behind insight—because that is where most honest readers live. If you need polished certainty, there are shorter videos elsewhere. If you need a thoughtful companion while you learn to hold complexity, stay.

You do not owe the internet a polished arc every week. You owe your practice a steadier honesty than the feed usually allows—and that honesty is what I am inviting when I keep these essays long enough to admit doubt without losing spine.

## Ethics, lineage, and the long view

Tarot does not float above history. The images carry aesthetic lineages, esoteric arguments, colonial entanglements, and living communities who have kept practice alive through censorship, ridicule, and revival. Acknowledging that inheritance is not an exercise in guilt; it is a way to read with wider eyes and lighter hands. When we know where a symbol came from, we are less likely to weaponise it—or to mistake our personal projection for ancient authority. Long-form writing has room to name teachers, credit sources, and point toward primary texts when a claim is not purely experiential.

Ethics, for me, begins with humility. Intuitive reading is not permission to override another person's agency. It is not a substitute for medical, legal, or financial counsel. It is a symbolic language that can clarify values, illuminate blind spots, and suggest the next honest question—nothing more, nothing less. When intuition is framed that modestly, it becomes more trustworthy, not less. The reader who refuses to play god in the chair often finds that clients relax, because they are no longer performing desperation for a saviour.

Forty-nine years with the cards has not made me infallible; it has made me quicker to notice when certainty is a defence. The Matakite strand in my whakapapa emphasises listening across thresholds—between waking and dream, between self and ancestors, between what is spoken and what is only sensed. I bring that forward carefully, aware that lineage language can be misread as exclusivity. My aim is simpler: to honour the practices that formed me while keeping the door open for anyone willing to do the slow work of ethical intuitive reading, whatever their background.

The Deck Compass is built as a subscription platform precisely because sustainable teaching requires boundaries: moderated space, curated curricula, and fair compensation for the people who hold the container. This library remains public so ideas can travel—indexed, cited, argued with, improved. If you are already certain tarot will stay central to your life, subscribing is one way to stay close to what we build. If you are still testing the depth of your commitment, these essays will remain here, ungated, as long as I am able to tend them.

## How to use this library

Treat these pages like a shelf you return to rather than a feed you finish. Bookmark what stings slightly; friction often marks the edge of a growing edge. If you keep a tarot journal, try copying one sentence that refuses to let you go and building a three-card spread around it the following week. That small loop—read, note, spread, reflect—is one of the cheapest ways to turn reading into craft without buying another deck you do not yet need.

Use the table of contents on longer pieces to jump between sections, then read linearly when you have time. When you encounter a horizontal rule, treat it as a breath: stand, stretch, notice what landed. Save articles that irritate you as well as the ones that soothe; sometimes the piece you resist is the one working on a question you have not yet named. Share work with friends who are past the beginner pamphlet stage but not yet hunting academic jargon—readers who want nuance without pretence.

If you teach elsewhere, link here as a companion rather than a competitor. The web needs more slow resources that assume adults can handle ambiguity. When you cite an essay, name the site and the author; when you disagree, do it in public with specifics so the conversation can deepen. I will revise pieces when better language or fairer framing becomes clear, and I will note substantive updates when they matter for practice.

Above all, read at the pace your nervous system can hold. Intuition deepens in repetition, not in volume. The tide returns whether you watch it or not; the question is whether you are standing on the shore when it does, notebook in hand, willing to be changed by what washes up. May this library give you a steadier place to stand—and may your practice, wherever it leads, stay honest, embodied, and kind. When you close the tab, carry one question forward that the cards did not answer yet; curiosity kept warm is the same fire that keeps a reader trustworthy across decades.
